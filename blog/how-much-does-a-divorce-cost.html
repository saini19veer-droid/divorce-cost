How Much Does a Divorce Actually Cost in 2026?

The answer ranges from $500 to $50,000+. Here's what actually determines where you land on that range.

DivorceCostBreakdown — July 2026 — 8 min read

The number you'll see most often online is $15,000. That's the average cost of a divorce in the United States, and it's about as useful as knowing the average temperature of a hospital. It tells you almost nothing about your specific situation.

The real range is $500 to $50,000 or more, and where you fall depends almost entirely on two things: whether you and your spouse can agree, and which state you're in.

The two types of divorce and what they cost

Every divorce is either contested or uncontested. This single variable is the biggest cost driver of everything that follows.

Uncontested divorce means you and your spouse agree on everything — property division, debt, child custody, child support, alimony. You hand the court a done deal and they formalize it.

Contested divorce means you disagree on one or more things and need the legal process — and eventually, maybe a judge — to sort it out. Attorney hours add up fast when there is conflict.

Those are per-spouse numbers. A contested divorce with kids that costs $25,000 per side is a $50,000 total family expense — money that in many cases comes directly out of assets that would otherwise be divided between the two of you.

What you are actually paying for

Court filing fees

These are fixed costs set by your state court system. They range from about $80 in Wyoming to over $400 in California. Filing fees are the same whether your divorce is simple or complicated — you pay them either way.

Attorney fees

This is where the money goes in contested divorces. Family law attorneys charge by the hour and require a retainer upfront — typically $2,500 to $10,000 — that they draw from as they work. In most major US cities, attorneys charge $250-$450 per hour. In NYC and San Francisco, $400-$600 is common.

An uncontested divorce might require 5-10 hours of attorney time on each side. A contested divorce involving custody and a house can run 40-100+ hours per attorney. Do that math and you understand where the $30,000 figure comes from.

Mediation

If you cannot agree but do not want to go to court, mediation is the middle option. A neutral mediator helps you negotiate. Costs run $150-$300 per hour, and sessions typically last 3-8 hours spread over multiple meetings. Total mediation costs usually land between $2,000 and $8,000 — much less than full litigation.

Child custody evaluations

When custody is contested and a judge orders a professional evaluation, you are looking at $2,000-$10,000 for a custody evaluator's report. This is one of the costs that surprises people most because it is not something you can control once a judge orders it.

How children change the math

Children do not just add emotional complexity — they add specific legal processes that cost money. Child support calculations, parenting plan negotiations, and custody arrangements all require additional attorney time even in relatively cooperative divorces.

The biggest cost variable The single biggest predictor of total divorce cost is not which state you are in or how many assets you have — it is how much you and your spouse disagree. Every hour of disagreement costs both of you attorney fees. Every hour you spend in agreement costs nothing.

The self-help option

If your divorce is truly uncontested — no children, minimal shared assets, both parties agree on everything — you can potentially handle it using your state's court forms. Many courts have self-help centers for exactly this purpose. Total cost: $80-$450 depending on state.

Online divorce services ($300-$800) are another option for simple uncontested cases — they prepare the paperwork for you and walk you through the process without the cost of a full attorney.
