Contested vs. Uncontested Divorce: The Cost Difference Is Staggering

Two couples, same state, same assets. One spent $3,200 total. The other spent $67,000. Here's what made the difference.

DivorceCostBreakdown — July 2026 — 7 min read

The terminology sounds legal and technical. Contested. Uncontested. What it actually means is simple: did you agree, or did you not?

If you agreed on everything — the house, the retirement accounts, the kids' schedule, who pays what debt — your divorce is uncontested. The legal process is mostly paperwork. Costs are low.

If you did not agree on one or more significant things, your divorce is contested. Every unresolved issue becomes a negotiation conducted by lawyers billing by the hour, and sometimes ends in a courtroom with a judge deciding for you. Costs are high.

The gap between these two outcomes is genuinely large.

Why attorney costs explode in contested divorces

Attorneys charge by the hour. An uncontested divorce requires maybe 5-10 hours of legal work per side. At $300/hour, that is $1,500-$3,000 per spouse.

A contested divorce is a different situation. When you cannot agree on the family home and child custody, your attorney sends letters, their attorney responds. Discovery happens. Depositions if things get serious. Negotiation sessions, each requiring attorney prep time. Possibly a court hearing. Possibly a trial.

Forty to one hundred attorney hours per side is not unusual in a contested divorce with real assets or custody disputes. At $300/hour, that is $12,000-$30,000 per attorney. Both spouses pay their own attorney.

The math people forget Fighting over a $40,000 retirement account split — if each of you spends $15,000 in attorney fees to litigate it — means you have both lost money. You would have been better off agreeing to give the other person the whole account. This happens more than people realize.

What causes a contested divorce

Most contested divorces are contested because:

  • Emotions are running high and one or both parties is not thinking clearly about financial outcomes
  • One spouse is using the legal process to punish the other or delay the inevitable
  • There is a genuine disagreement about child custody that requires outside resolution
  • There are complex assets — a business, significant real estate, stock options — that require valuation and negotiation

The mediation middle ground

If you cannot agree but also cannot afford a full contested divorce, mediation is the path most people do not explore enough. A mediator is a neutral third party — often a retired judge or experienced family law attorney — who helps you and your spouse negotiate toward agreement.

Mediation costs $150-$300 per hour. Most divorces resolve in 3-6 sessions. Total: $3,000-$8,000. Far less than litigation. And unlike a court proceeding, you control the outcome — a judge does not decide for you.
